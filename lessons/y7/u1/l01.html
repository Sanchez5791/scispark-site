Everything you can touch

Pick up a piece of bread. It is real — you can hold it, you can feel it is there.
Now hold a balloon. It takes up space, even though it feels almost empty.
Both have something in common — and that something is called matter.

matter
mass
space
substance

The 4 words for today

matter (物质)
anything that has some amount of stuff inside and takes up space
mass (质量)
the amount of matter (stuff) in something, measured in kilograms (kg)
space (空间)
the room something takes up (its volume)
substance (物体)
a single kind of stuff (water, iron, air)
Screen 2 · Learn

Learn

Concept

What does it mean to have mass?

[A] Look at one lego brick. Now look at a pile of 100 lego bricks stuck together. The pile has more stuff inside it than the single brick — more pieces packed in.
[B] Like a half-empty water bottle versus a fully-filled water bottle — the full one has more water inside, more amount of stuff. We don't say "heavier" — we say "more stuff".
[C] Mass is the amount of matter in something. The more stuff inside, the bigger the mass. We measure mass in kilograms (kg).

Definition

Mass = the amount of matter (stuff) inside something. Measured in kilograms (kg).

Few lego pieces in a pile = small amount of matter = small mass
Many lego pieces in a pile = lots of matter = bigger mass
Key idea

Mass is NOT "how heavy" something is. "Heavy" is about gravity pulling — we will learn that later (Year 7 Forces). Mass is about HOW MUCH stuff is inside.

Matter also takes up space

[A] Blow up a balloon. As it gets bigger, it pushes the air around it out of the way.
[B] Like when you sit down on a full sofa, you have to push your friend over to make room — that "make room" feeling is taking up space.
[C] Everything made of matter takes up some space. We call this taking up volume.

Definition

Space (volume) = the room something takes up. Even invisible matter like air takes up space.

A balloon pushes air around it → it takes up space
Water in a glass — the water takes the glass's shape and fills its volume
Key idea

All matter has mass AND takes up space. Both, not just one. If something has mass but no volume, or volume but no mass — it's not matter.

Matter vs material — what's the difference?

[A] You see a tree growing in a forest. The tree just stands there — no one is using it for anything. That tree is matter (it has mass + takes up space).
[B] Like flour sitting in your kitchen — flour is just stuff (matter) until someone uses it to bake bread. Once it's used for a purpose, we give it a different name: we call it material.
[C] When someone cuts the tree and uses the wood to build a chair, the wood is now a material (it has a use). The chair itself is matter again (it has mass + space). So: every material is matter, but not every matter is a material — only when it has a purpose.

Definition

Material = matter that has a purpose or use. Every material is matter, but not every matter is a material.

Tree in forest = matter (just exists, no use yet)
Wood from cut tree = material (has a purpose: build things)
Wooden chair = matter again (and a product made from the material)
Key idea

The same wood can be matter AND material at the same time. The difference is whether it has a use or not. Both still take up space and have mass.

Q1
Guided question
1 mark

Look at these three things: a glass of water, a metal knife, and a rubber eraser. Which of them are matter? Tick all that apply.

Saved
Hint

Use the two-question test on each item: (1) does it have mass? (you can weigh it) (2) does it take up space? If both yes → it's matter. Liquids, solids, gases — all can be matter.

Expected answer

All three are matter. Each one has mass (you can weigh it) and takes up space (it has volume).

If your answer matches

Spot on. State (liquid/solid/etc.) doesn't decide whether something is matter — having mass + taking up space does.

If not yet

Close. Remember: matter is anything with mass that takes up space — solid, liquid, gas, all count. Try the two-question test on each item again.

Q2
Guided question
2 marks

A balloon looks "empty" because you can't see anything inside, but the air inside still weighs a tiny bit and takes up space. Is the air inside the balloon matter? Explain in one sentence.

Saved
Hint

Use the two-question test: (1) does air have mass? (2) does air take up space? If both yes → it's matter. Just because you can't see something doesn't mean it isn't there.

Expected answer

Yes, the air is matter, because it has mass (a tiny weight) and it takes up space (fills the balloon).

If your answer matches

Yes — and that's a big idea. Matter isn't just things you can see. Air, even though invisible, is still matter.

If not yet

Look again — the question already tells you the air "weighs a tiny bit" and "takes up space". Both conditions for matter are met, so the answer is yes.

Q3
Guided question
2 marks

A tree grows in a garden. Someone cuts the tree and uses the wood to make a chair. Look at the three stages: tree → wood → chair.

(a) In the forest, before anyone touched it, the tree is _______ (matter or material?). [1 mark]
(b) The wood used to make the chair is called _______ (matter or material?). [1 mark]

Saved
Hint
  • Ask yourself: is it being used for a purpose yet?
  • Yes → material
  • No, it's just there → matter
  • Tree in forest = no use yet. Wood for chair = has a use now.
Expected answer

(a) matter — the tree has mass and takes up space but has no use yet. (b) material — the wood is now used for a purpose (making a chair).

If your answer matches

Exactly. Same wood, two names — depends on whether it has a purpose. This is the key idea: material = matter with a job to do.

If not yet

Almost. The two names (matter / material) are not about what something is, they are about whether it has a use. Tree just standing = matter. Wood being used = material. Try again.

Q4
Solo question
1 mark

Rajiv fills a balloon with helium gas. He puts the balloon inside a tightly sealed box. The box also contains air around the balloon.

The balloon is popped. The helium gas spreads through the box and mixes with the air.

What happens to the mass of the helium? Does it increase, decrease, or stay the same?

Saved
Expected answer

The mass of helium stays the same.

If your answer matches

Correct. Mass is the amount of matter. The helium didn't disappear — it just spread out into the box. The amount of helium stuff is the same, so the mass stays the same.

If not yet

Almost. Think: did any helium leave the box (which is sealed)? Did the helium turn into something else? No — it just spread out. The same amount of helium is still there, so the mass stays the same.

Q5
Solo question
1 mark

Blessy ties two balloons of the same size (both filled with air) to the two ends of a horizontal bar, and balances them. The balloons hang from the bar — one on each side.

Blessy pushes a pin into the right-side balloon. The balloon bursts and all the air inside escapes. The other balloon (still full of air) is not touched.

The white balloon (left side) moves downwards — the bar tilts.

What property of air does this model show?

Saved
Expected answer

Air has mass.

If your answer matches

Yes. When the right balloon bursts, the air escapes — so the right side becomes lighter. That means the air inside had mass to begin with. This is the proof: air is matter, with mass.

If not yet

Think about what changed when the right balloon burst. The bar was balanced before (both sides had air). After bursting, one side lost its air and became lighter — so the bar tilted. That tilt only happens if the air had mass to start with.

What we learned today

"Heavy" is NOT mass — heavy is about gravity pulling (we learn that later in Year 7 Forces). Mass is about how MUCH stuff is inside.
Space is the room something takes up (its volume).
Air is matter (you can't see it, but it has mass and takes up space).
Material is matter with a purpose or use. Every material is matter, but not every matter is a material.
